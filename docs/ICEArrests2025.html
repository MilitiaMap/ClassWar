Overview of Aggressive Detention and Arrest Practices by US Immigration and Customs Enforcement and Customs and Border Protection in 2025

1. Executive Summary:

This report examines the detention and arrest practices of U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) and Customs and Border Protection (CBP) in 2025, with a particular focus on allegations of aggressive tactics and the reported abductions of students involved in pro-Palestine protests. The analysis of news articles, reports from human rights organizations, official statements, and legal documents published in 2025 reveals a complex landscape of immigration enforcement under the new administration. Key findings include a reported increase in ICE arrests during the early months of 2025 compared to the previous fiscal year 1, alongside the resumption and expansion of family detention 3. Notably, several incidents of alleged abductions of students participating in pro-Palestine protests on university campuses have been reported, raising significant concerns about the targeting of political expression 10. Policy changes, such as the rescission of protected areas policies, have broadened the scope of potential enforcement actions 33. These developments have prompted legal challenges and drawn strong criticism from human rights organizations regarding potential violations of constitutional and human rights 3.

2. Introduction:

The U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), under the Department of Homeland Security (DHS), is primarily responsible for enforcing immigration laws within the interior of the United States, focusing on the identification, apprehension, detention, and removal of individuals unlawfully present in the country 75. U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP), also part of DHS, is tasked with securing the nation's borders, which includes preventing illegal immigration and apprehending individuals attempting to enter the country unlawfully between ports of entry 76.

The year 2025 witnessed significant shifts in immigration policy and enforcement practices following the change in presidential administration. Executive orders issued in January 2025 signaled a move towards more stringent border security measures and increased interior enforcement 40. These policy changes set the stage for a potentially more aggressive approach to immigration enforcement by both ICE and CBP. This report specifically examines the reported detention and arrest practices of these agencies in 2025, paying close attention to allegations of aggressive tactics. A key area of focus is the reported abductions of students involved in protests, as these incidents raise serious questions about the intersection of immigration enforcement, civil liberties, and freedom of expression. By analyzing available information from news media, human rights organizations, official statements from ICE and CBP, and legal documents published in 2025, this report aims to provide a comprehensive overview of these practices and identify any emerging trends or human rights concerns.

3. Overview of Reported Detention Practices in 2025:

  • 3.1. ICE Detention Practices:
  • The detention practices of ICE in 2025 have been shaped by a significant legal development, the settlement agreement in Gonzalez v. ICE, which came into effect in March 2025 42. Historically, ICE has frequently utilized "detainers," which are requests to local law enforcement agencies to hold individuals for an additional 48 hours after their release from local custody so that ICE can take them into immigration detention 42. Prior to the settlement, there was no requirement for neutral oversight to establish probable cause for these detainer arrests 42. The Gonzalez v. ICE settlement introduces changes to this practice, particularly concerning detainers issued by the Pacific Enforcement Response Center (PERC), which operates after business hours and covers 42 states 42. Unless an individual has a prior deportation order or is already in removal proceedings, PERC is now restricted from issuing detainers without undergoing a neutral review process 42. Instead of detainers, PERC is primarily permitted to issue a modified form called a "Request for Notification of Release," which explicitly states that local agencies do not have the authority to continue holding an individual for ICE purposes beyond their scheduled release 42. This means that during the 12 hours per day when ICE relies on PERC, jails in the majority of U.S. states will only receive requests to notify ICE of a person's pending release and will be informed that they cannot hold individuals for ICE beyond their release date 42. A concern remains, however, that ICE might attempt to circumvent these restrictions by transferring PERC's investigation and detainer responsibilities to other ICE offices without implementing a neutral review process 42. The Gonzalez v. ICE settlement marks a significant step towards addressing constitutional concerns related to ICE detainers by mandating a neutral review process for most detainer issuances in a large portion of the United States 42. However, the actual impact will depend on ICE's adherence to the terms and whether effective mechanisms are in place to prevent circumvention.
  • In March 2025, the American Immigration Lawyers Association (AILA) released a statement noting the resumption of family detention by the Trump administration, with families being detained in facilities in Karnes County and Dilley, Texas 3. This practice, where families with children are held together in detention pending their court proceedings, has drawn criticism from various health and human rights organizations 4. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) joined other leading health care organizations in calling for an end to family detention, emphasizing that no amount of time in detention is safe for children and that it poses a threat to their health, potentially causing psychological trauma and long-term mental health risks 4. Studies of detained immigrants have shown that children and parents may suffer negative physical and emotional symptoms, including anxiety, depression, and posttraumatic stress disorder 4. Parents in detention centers have reported regressive behavioral changes in their children, such as decreased eating, sleep disturbances, clinginess, withdrawal, self-injurious behavior, and aggression 4. Furthermore, there have been reports of the Trump administration considering the use of military bases, including Fort Bliss in Texas, to hold detained non-citizens 48. This plan has raised concerns about the legal authority for such detentions, the minimum standards that would be required for detention conditions, and the provision of access to legal counsel 48. The renewed emphasis on family detention in 2025 represents a concerning shift in immigration enforcement practices, potentially jeopardizing the well-being of children and families seeking refuge in the United States.
  • Data from various sources indicates a significant increase in ICE detention in early 2025. According to the National Network for Immigrant and Refugee Rights (NNIRR), as of March 9, 2025, ICE was detaining 46,269 people, the highest number since October 2019 3. This figure, while below the pre-pandemic levels of the first Trump administration, shows a marked increase from the 39,703 individuals detained on January 22, 2025 3. Notably, almost half of those in ICE detention, 23,081 individuals, had no criminal record as of March 9, 2025 45. A report by TRAC (Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse) indicates that ICE detention grew by 4,500 people (12%) in the first month under the new administration, from January 25 to February 23, 2025 85. Congress has provided funding for ICE to detain a daily average of 41,500 noncitizens in FY2024, with projections of increasing detention capacity requiring further funding 3. There have also been concerns raised regarding the transparency of ICE detention numbers at facilities located at Guantanamo Bay, with reported average daily populations seeming to contradict press reports of larger numbers of individuals being detained there 45. The trend of increasing detention, particularly of individuals with no criminal record, raises questions about the necessity and appropriateness of such measures.
  • ICE also utilizes Alternatives to Detention (ATD) programs to monitor individuals outside of traditional detention facilities. In FY2024, Congress provided approximately $470 million in funding for these programs, with 194,427 people enrolled in FY2023 3. As of March 7, 2025, ICE's ATD programs were monitoring 183,784 families and single individuals 45. Despite the existence and funding of these alternatives, the significant increase in detention numbers suggests that ICE is prioritizing detention over these less restrictive and potentially more humane options 3.
  • 3.2. CBP Detention Practices:
  • CBP reported a significant decrease in encounters with individuals at the U.S.-Mexico border in early 2025. In February 2025, U.S. Border Patrol (USBP) nationwide apprehensions averaged approximately 330 per day, the lowest nationwide average in CBP history 6. Southwest border apprehensions plunged to less than 300 per day in February, with USBP apprehending 8,347 individuals crossing between ports of entry, a 71% decrease from January 2025 and a 94% decrease from February 2024 6. CBP's Office of Field Operations (OFO) also encountered 3,362 inadmissible individuals at ports of entry along the southwest border in February 2025, representing a 90% decrease from January 2025 and a 93% decrease from February 2024 6. This dramatic reduction in border encounters suggests a significant impact of the new administration's policies aimed at halting illegal immigration 6.
  • Despite the decrease in overall border encounters, CBP has been involved in the resumption of family detention alongside ICE. The Trump administration plans to reopen a facility in Dilley, Texas, to hold families, which would add space for up to 2,400 people 5. The South Texas Family Residential Center in Dilley has been repurposed to detain migrant families facing deportation 5. This move has been met with alarm by immigrant rights advocates 5.
  • CBP launched the CBP Home mobile application in 2025, allowing individuals unlawfully present in the U.S. or those with revoked parole to voluntarily notify the government of their intent to depart 6. This app is intended to comply with Executive Order 14159, "Protecting the American People Against Invasion," by providing an orderly process for communicating departure plans 6. The administration has conveyed a clear message that voluntary departure now offers a chance to return and live the American dream, while those who do not leave voluntarily will be found, deported, and permanently barred from reentry 6.

4. Analysis of Reported Arrest Practices in 2025:

  • 4.1. ICE Arrest Practices:
  • Secretary of Homeland Security Kristi Noem announced in March 2025 that ICE had made 32,809 enforcement arrests in the first 50 days of the Trump administration 1. This figure reportedly exceeded the 33,242 at-large arrests made by ICE's Enforcement and Removal Operations (ERO) in the entire fiscal year 2024 1. Of those arrested in the first 50 days, nearly half (14,111) were convicted criminals, and about a third (9,980) had pending criminal charges 1. ICE also reported arresting 1,155 criminal gang members and 39 known or suspected terrorists during this period 1. However, an analysis by TRAC of ICE data through February 8, 2025, indicated that the average daily number of ICE arrests (724) during the first eight days of February was actually 4.7 percent lower than the average daily number of 759 arrests during all of FY 2024 under the Biden administration 89. This suggests a discrepancy between the administration's initial claims of a surge in arrests and more detailed statistical analysis over a slightly later period.
  • ICE conducted various enforcement operations in 2025. In March, ICE agents raided an industrial paint business in El Cajon, California, suspected of knowingly hiring workers without legal status, arresting fewer than 20 people 91. While a federal warrant was obtained for the search, workers reported that dozens of ICE and Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) agents handcuffed more than 50 employees and detained them to verify their immigration status 91. ICE also conducted a week-long multi-agency operation in the Rio Grande Valley in March, resulting in the arrest of 72 illegal aliens, including 61 criminal aliens and two documented gang members 92. These operations often involve collaboration with federal, state, and local law enforcement partners 92. In Mississippi, a joint operation between the Rankin County Sheriff's Department and CBP in March led to the arrest of 25 undocumented immigrants traveling through the state along the Interstate 20 corridor 95.
  • The arrest practices of ICE in 2025 have faced allegations of being unlawful and conducted without proper warrants. The ACLU of Illinois and the National Immigrant Justice Center (NIJC) announced a federal court action in March on behalf of 22 people, including a U.S. citizen, who were allegedly subjected to unlawful arrests and detention by ICE in the early weeks of the new administration 6. The lawsuit alleges that these arrests violated the Fourth Amendment and a 2022 federal court settlement that restricted ICE from making warrantless arrests 6. U.S. Senator Mark R. Warner (D-VA) also wrote to DHS and ICE leadership regarding the alarming immigration arrest of a U.S. citizen and Virginia resident in March, who was stopped and interrogated despite providing identification 96. The ACLU lawsuit highlighted instances of "collateral arrests," where ICE officers detained individuals who were present during targeted operations but were not the primary targets themselves 6. These allegations raise significant concerns about potential violations of constitutional rights during ICE arrest practices in 2025.
  • 4.2. CBP Arrest Practices:
  • CBP officers have been active in arresting individuals attempting to smuggle undocumented immigrants into the United States. In March 2025, CBP officers at the Cannon Corners border crossing in Champlain, N.Y., arrested a Canadian citizen attempting to conceal an undocumented individual in the spare tire area of a vehicle 97. This incident highlights the ongoing efforts by CBP to combat human smuggling at the border 97.
  • CBP's arrest practices in 2025 have also been challenged with allegations of racial profiling and unlawful conduct during interior enforcement operations. In February 2025, the United Farm Workers (UFW) and five Kern County residents filed a lawsuit against DHS, CBP, and U.S. Border Patrol in response to "Operation Return to Sender," a weeklong operation in January that targeted predominantly Latino areas of Kern County, California 42. The lawsuit alleges that Border Patrol agents indiscriminately stopped and arrested people of color who appeared to be farmworkers or day laborers, regardless of their actual immigration status or individual circumstances, suggesting racial profiling 44. It further claims that individuals were coerced into signing forms for "voluntary departure" without being allowed to read them or understand their rights, and that agents retaliated against those who tried to exercise their rights or presented lawful immigration status by arresting them anyway 42. The lawsuit argues that these practices violated the plaintiffs' Fourth and Fifth Amendment rights 44.
  • CBP also reported arrests of individuals with criminal records. In March 2025, Border Patrol agents from the Miami Sector arrested seven illegal aliens in the Florida Keys, all of whom had extensive criminal records, including convictions for elder abuse, methamphetamine trafficking, oxycodone trafficking, and driving under the influence causing serious injury 94. In a separate incident in March, Del Rio Sector Border Patrol agents arrested three individuals near Carrizo Springs, Texas, one of whom had been previously deported in 2013 and faced charges for reentry after deportation 100. These arrests indicate CBP's continued focus on apprehending and processing individuals with criminal histories and prior immigration violations.

5. In-Depth Examination of Alleged Abductions of Students at Protests:

  • 5.1. Case Study: Rumeysa Ozturk (Tufts University):
  • Rumeysa Ozturk, a 30-year-old Turkish national and doctoral student at Tufts University with a valid F-1 student visa, was arrested on the evening of March 25, 2025, while on her way to meet friends for an iftar dinner during Ramadan 10. The arrest was carried out by six plainclothes individuals, some of whom were masked and traveling in unmarked vehicles, near her home in Somerville, Massachusetts 10. Surveillance video of the incident shows the agents approaching Ozturk, handcuffing her, and taking her away in an SUV, an event described by a witness as resembling a kidnapping 10. Tufts University later stated that Ozturk's visa had been terminated 10. Her arrest came shortly after she was doxxed by a Zionist vigilante group, Canary Mission, apparently due to an op-ed she co-authored in the student newspaper criticizing Tufts' response to pro-Palestine protests and urging the university to divest from Israel 11. The Department of Homeland Security claimed, without providing evidence, that Ozturk was arrested because she "engaged in activities in support of Hamas" 12. Despite a federal judge issuing an order that Ozturk should not be moved outside of Massachusetts without 48 hours' notice, she was transferred to the South Louisiana ICE Processing Center 10. Lawmakers, including Representatives Ayanna Pressley, Elizabeth Warren, and Edward Markey, strongly condemned her arrest, calling it a "disturbing arrest and detention" and demanding an explanation from the administration, her release, and the restoration of her visa 10.
  • 5.2. Case Study: Alireza Doroudi (University of Alabama):
  • Alireza Doroudi, a 22-year-old Iranian doctoral student in mechanical engineering at the University of Alabama, was detained by ICE at his off-campus home at 5 a.m. on Tuesday, March 26, 2025 12. The University of Alabama confirmed his detention by federal immigration authorities 12. The Department of Homeland Security stated that his arrest was made in accordance with the State Department's revocation of his student visa due to "significant national security risk concerns," although specific details were not provided 12. Doroudi was initially held at Pickens County Jail, and while jail records did not list any charges against him, there were reports that ICE detainees at this facility are typically transferred to a detention facility in Louisiana 12. According to a message shared among Iranian students at the university, Doroudi entered the U.S. in January 2023 on an F-1 student visa that was reportedly revoked six months later 12. The National Iranian American Council suggested that Doroudi's arrest was a retaliation against his speech and activism against war 12.
  • 5.3. Case Study: Mahmoud Khalil (Columbia University):
  • Mahmoud Khalil, a Palestinian graduate of Columbia University and a lawful permanent resident (green card holder), was arrested by ICE agents at his home on March 8, 2025 18. Khalil was involved in pro-Palestine protests at Columbia University in the spring of 2024 and was described as a leader in the university's pro-Palestine movement 18. The Trump administration alleged that his arrest was in support of executive orders prohibiting anti-Semitism and claimed he engaged in activities aligned with Hamas 22. Despite holding a green card and having no criminal charges filed against him, Khalil remained in ICE custody in Louisiana as of late March 18. His attorneys filed a habeas corpus petition challenging his detention as a violation of his First Amendment rights and due process 24. A judge ruled that his case should be heard in New Jersey, rejecting the government's attempt to transfer it to Louisiana 18.
  • 5.4. Other Reported Cases:
  • Yunseo Chung, a legal permanent resident and student at Columbia University, was also targeted by ICE in March 2025 due to her participation in pro-Palestine protests 12. Badar Khan Suri, an Indian citizen studying and teaching at Georgetown University on a valid J-1 visa, was detained by ICE in March for allegedly "spreading Hamas propaganda" 12. Alfredo “Lelo” Juarez Zeferino, a farmworker union leader and immigrant rights activist in Washington state, was arrested by ICE in late March, sparking protests 20. Ranjani Srinivasan, a doctoral candidate at Columbia University from India, had her student visa revoked and self-deported using the CBP Home app in March after being accused of supporting Hamas 22.

6. Official Statements and Policy Changes by ICE and CBP in 2025:

  • DHS Secretary Kristi Noem made several public statements in early 2025 emphasizing a stricter approach to immigration enforcement. She announced a significant increase in ICE arrests in the first 50 days of the administration and reiterated the commitment to deporting known terrorists, cartel members, and gang members 1. Secretary Noem also stressed the administration's intention to enforce all immigration laws and end "catch-and-release" policies, ensuring that individuals apprehended for immigration violations are detained until their removal 79. She announced the termination of the CBP One app, which had been used to schedule appointments for asylum seekers at the border 105. Her statements consistently conveyed a message of prioritizing border security and national safety through robust enforcement measures 104.
  • ICE press releases in 2025 primarily focused on enforcement actions targeting individuals with criminal histories and those subject to removal orders. Examples include the removal of a man wanted in El Salvador for murder 93, the arrest of an illegal Mexican national involved in a vehicular homicide 93, and multi-agency operations resulting in the arrests of dozens of "criminal aliens" 92. These releases often highlight the collaboration between ICE and other federal, state, and local law enforcement agencies 92.
  • CBP news releases in early 2025 emphasized the significant decrease in border encounters and apprehensions following the implementation of new executive orders 6. CBP also reported arrests related to drug and human smuggling 107 and announced the launch of the CBP Home app to facilitate voluntary departures 6. Acting CBP Commissioner Pete Flores stated that the agency was aggressively implementing the President's Executive Orders to secure the borders, resulting in historic lows in border apprehensions 6.
  • Significant policy changes occurred in 2025 regarding immigration enforcement at sensitive locations. On January 20, 2025, the Trump administration rescinded a Biden-era policy that had generally prevented ICE and CBP agents from making arrests at schools, places of worship, and hospitals without prior authorization from agency headquarters 33. This rescission gave ICE agents broader authority to take enforcement actions in these previously protected areas 33. ICE issued interim guidance on January 21, 2025, regarding enforcement actions in or near courthouses, generally advising officers to avoid such actions in non-public areas like family court 34. By March 2025, ICE was under a court order related to enforcement actions in or near places of worship from a specified list of locations 34. For university campuses, the rescission meant they no longer had special protection from immigration enforcement 35. Institutions were advised to establish protocols for responding to ICE presence on campus, including verifying credentials and warrants before granting access to non-public areas 35.

7. Perspectives from Human Rights Organizations and Advocacy Groups:

Human rights organizations and advocacy groups have voiced significant concerns regarding the reported detention and arrest practices of ICE and CBP in 2025. The resumption of family detention has been widely criticized for its detrimental impact on the well-being of children, with groups like AILA, AAP, and Amnesty International highlighting the potential for trauma and long-term harm 3. Allegations of unlawful and warrantless arrests by ICE and CBP have prompted legal challenges and strong condemnations from organizations such as the ACLU and NIJC, who argue that these practices violate constitutional rights 6. The targeting of students involved in pro-Palestine protests has been particularly alarming, with concerns raised that immigration enforcement is being used to suppress free speech and political dissent on university campuses 10. The increase in ICE detention numbers, coupled with the fact that nearly half of detainees have no criminal record, has led to questions about the necessity and justification for such widespread detention 3. The ACLU has also expressed concerns about the potential expansion of immigration detention nationwide 54. Human rights groups fear that the increased enforcement and the rescission of protected areas policies will have a chilling effect on immigrant communities, making individuals afraid to access essential services like healthcare and education 33.

8. Legal Challenges and Lawsuits Related to 2025 Practices:

Several legal challenges have been filed in 2025 concerning the detention and arrest practices of ICE and CBP. The Gonzalez v. ICE settlement, effective March 2025, aims to reform ICE detainer practices by requiring neutral review in many cases 42. The ACLU of Illinois and NIJC filed a lawsuit in March challenging alleged unlawful warrantless arrests by ICE in the Midwest, seeking the release of detained individuals and accountability for the agency's actions 6. The United Farm Workers (UFW) and Bakersfield residents sued DHS, CBP, and Border Patrol in February over "Operation Return to Sender," alleging racial profiling and unlawful arrests in Kern County, California 42. Habeas corpus petitions were filed on behalf of Columbia University graduate Mahmoud Khalil and Georgetown University scholar Badar Khan Suri, challenging their detention by ICE as violations of their constitutional rights, particularly the First Amendment 17. A lawsuit was also reportedly filed on behalf of Columbia University student Yunseo Chung, who was targeted for her involvement in pro-Palestine protests 17. These legal actions highlight the significant concerns surrounding the legality and constitutionality of the immigration enforcement practices in 2025.

9. Comparative Analysis and Trends:

Comparing ICE arrest and removal numbers in early 2025 with data from FY 2024 reveals some discrepancies. While Secretary Noem announced a substantial increase in arrests in the first 50 days of the new administration 1, TRAC's analysis of February data indicated a slight decrease in the daily average of arrests compared to FY 2024 under the previous administration 89. ICE detention numbers showed a clear upward trend in early 2025, increasing from the end of the Biden administration 81. In contrast, CBP reported a dramatic decrease in border apprehensions in January and February 2025 compared to the same period in 2024 6. Notably, the practice of family detention, which had been reduced under the Biden administration, was resumed and planned for expansion in 2025 3. These trends suggest a shift in enforcement focus, with decreased border encounters but potentially intensified interior enforcement in certain areas, including a concerning emphasis on detention and the targeting of activists.

10. Conclusion and Recommendations:

The analysis of reported detention and arrest practices by ICE and CBP in 2025 reveals a number of concerning trends. The alleged abductions of students involved in pro-Palestine protests raise serious questions about the potential use of immigration enforcement to suppress political expression, impacting fundamental rights such as freedom of speech and academic freedom. The resumption and planned expansion of family detention, despite the known harms to children, indicate a move towards more punitive measures. Allegations of unlawful arrests, warrantless stops, and racial profiling continue to plague both agencies, as evidenced by the numerous legal challenges filed. While CBP reported a significant decrease in border encounters, ICE's interior enforcement appears to be intensifying in key areas, particularly concerning detention.

To address these issues, the following recommendations are made:

  • Reinstate and strengthen policies that protect sensitive locations, including schools, universities, hospitals, and places of worship, from immigration enforcement actions to ensure these remain safe and accessible to all members of the community.
  • Implement more robust oversight and accountability mechanisms for ICE and CBP arrest and detention practices, including the establishment of independent bodies to investigate allegations of abuse and misconduct.
  • Guarantee due process and access to legal representation for all individuals facing immigration detention or removal proceedings, recognizing the complexity of immigration law and the vulnerability of those in custody.
  • End the practice of family detention immediately and invest in community-based alternatives to detention that have proven to be more humane and cost-effective while ensuring compliance with immigration proceedings.
  • Uphold and protect the First Amendment rights of students and activists, ensuring that immigration enforcement powers are not misused to target individuals based on their political views or activism.
  • Promote greater transparency in ICE and CBP operations by requiring the regular release of comprehensive data on arrests, detentions, enforcement actions, and internal investigations, allowing for public scrutiny and informed policy debate.

Legal reforms are also necessary to strengthen protections against unlawful immigration enforcement practices. This includes clarifying and reinforcing the rights of non-citizens against arbitrary stops, searches, and arrests, as well as ensuring robust judicial review of immigration enforcement actions that may violate constitutional rights. Further research is needed to fully understand the long-term impacts of these evolving enforcement practices on immigrant communities and the broader society.
